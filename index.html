I am an astronomer, working at the European Southern Observatory, in Garching by Munich, Germany.

I investigate how the formation of stars affects galaxies, and, how the properties of galaxies affect star formation. To do this, I analyse both real and synthetic observations of the emission from interstellar gas in distant galaxies.

In addition to my research, I regularly give guided tours, planetarium shows and public talks about astronomy in English and German at the ESO Supernova. In the past I also developed workshops for high school students and helped organise stargazing nights.

Outside of academia, I love outdoor sports, particularly running and cycling. I spend a lot of time cooking and, to my friends' delight (or horror), I also like to draw portraits.

Below this little introduction, you'll find more information about my research. Feel free to contact me if you find it anywhere near as fascinating as I do.

Research

In the typical massive galaxies of 10 billion years ago, star formation was proceeding far more rapidly than in most galaxies today. Tens to hundreds of solar masses worth of stars were forming each year. In contrast, our own Milky Way is only forming about one Sun worth of stars per year. These highly star-forming distant galaxies were also far more gas-rich than picture-book local spirals or the Milky Way. So what has changed? How is this the gas out of which stars form distributed relative to the already-formed stars? Under what conditions is star formation taking place? These are some of the questions that my research tries to address.

Stars form predominantly from cold molecular gas, the dominant component of which, molecular hydrogen (H2) is practically invisible at the typical temperatures of star-forming gas. Astronomers like myself therefore rely on the emission from other, trace, components of the interstellar medium to infer the total amount and properties of star-forming gas. The molecular gas of distant galaxies is typically observed via either the rotational line emission from carbon monoxide (CO), or, the thermal emission from dust grains. Much of my recent work revolves around observing and understanding this emission.

To study the cold gas content of local galaxies, astronomers have mostly relied upon the ground transition of CO, i.e. CO(1-0). Observations of the CO(1-0) line are scaled to a molecular gas mass via a highly-debated correction factor. However, observing the CO(1-0) emission of distant galaxies is challenging and time-consuming. Instead higher-order transitions of CO are typically observed, which require an additional correction for the state of excitation of the gas. Determining the total molecular gas content from thermal dust emission is no less complicated. Dust can be used to trace the total gas content as it is well-mixed with gas in the interstellar medium and is a catalyst for the formation of H2. However, to convert the dust emission, measured at a single wavelength, to a molecular gas mass requires multiple assumptions on e.g. the average dust temperature, the dust-to-gas mass ratio and the dust properties (as encompassed by the description of the dust opacity).

During my PhD I investigated how to convert observations of the intensity and distribution of CO line and thermal dust emission to measurements of the total content and distribution of cold molecular gas. I analysed observations from three submillimetre-to-radio observatories; ALMA , NOEMA and the VLA. I also simulated the line emission from distant, molecular cloud complexes using the moving-mesh hydrodynamics code Arepo and the radiative transfer code POLARIS.

Using ALMA observations of thermal dust emission and VLA observations of CO(1-0) emission, I have compared how well the gas masses derived from these two sets of measurements agree for massive, star-forming galaxies at redshift 1.5 (seen as they were 9 billion years ago). Despite multiple assumptions, it seems that the thermal dust emission (at long rest-frame wavelengths) traces the total molecular gas content of massive galaxies as reliably as the traditional workhorse, CO(1-0), i.e. the difference is within a factor of 2. Outreach

One of the most enjoyable parts of my job is sharing my love of astronomy with the general public.

I have been doing outreach since my undergraduate studies, when I co-developed and regularly hosted two workshops for high school students; one general astrophysics workshop and one workshop in which students had to estimate the properties and habitability of exoplanets based on real data. During my Masters, I led stargazing evenings for a variety of groups to show them nebulae, star clusters and solar system objects.

Working with the Haus der Astronomie, I regularly gave telescope tours and planetarium shows for groups including school students, visiting academics, senior hiking groups, teams from industry and a nearby police department. In celebration of the 50 year anniversary of the first moon landing, I held a public talk about the role of Australian telescopes in the TV coverage and tracking of the NASA Apollo missions (link to event). As part of the Faszination Astronomie Online youtube channel, I gave a 30-minute talk (in German) about the History and Future of Star Formation in the Universe.
